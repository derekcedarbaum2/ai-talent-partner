# Cover letter best practices (senior and technical roles)

Synthesis of current hiring-manager and recruiter guidance, validated against an adversarial persona panel. Load before drafting.

## The one-line truth
At the senior level a cover letter is a tiebreaker and a signal device, not a gate. Recruiters mostly skim it (under 2 minutes); hiring managers at mission-driven and smaller companies actually read it, which is exactly where these letters are aimed. It earns its keep only by doing what the resume cannot: explain a transition, demonstrate company-specific judgment, or prove the candidate can think and write. Re-narrating the resume is dead weight.

## Length and shape
- 250 to 400 words. One page. 3 to 5 short paragraphs. Going long is the top failure mode; cut before adding.
- Vary rhythm. Short punch sentence, then a longer one. Avoid four long multi-clause sentences in a row.

## Opener
- Lead with a quantified accomplishment or a sharp, specific company or product insight.
- Banned openers: "I am writing to apply for", "I am excited to apply", "My name is". Recruiters have seen enough of these to wallpaper their homes.

## Structure that works
1. Hook: quantified result or company-specific insight that earns paragraph two.
2. Proof (the spine): one challenge to action to result story on the JD's core competency. This is the centerpiece; weight it heaviest.
3. Differentiator: the second-strongest thing, tied to an explicit JD bet (for example "this role bets on AI").
4. Why-this-company: genuine, evidence-backed, in the candidate's own words.
5. Close: confident, forward-looking. No throat-clearing, no stiff sign-off for startups.

## Specificity (the whole game)
- Numbers, names, dollar figures, dates beat any competency adjective.
- Isolate the candidate's contribution. "I led X decision; we shipped Y" beats "the work that led to $120M". Hard technical leaders read team-credit as borrowed credit.
- Name a decision with a cost (what got cut, who pushed back), not just wins. Product leaders read win-only stories as mid-level.

## Mirroring the JD
Mirror enough to hit ATS keywords and prove fit. Never parrot. Use the JD to pick which stories to tell, not to fill the letter with their language. Quoting the JD's exact phrasing back reads as parroting.

## The why-this-company paragraph
- Helps if specific; reads as filler if generic.
- "I'm passionate about your mission" is filler, filtered instantly.
- Specific product judgment or a demonstrated prior choice is signal.
- Never quote the company's own tagline or marketing back at them. It reads as pandering even when sincere. State the same conviction in the candidate's own words.

## Tone
Confident, not self-indulgent. Frame everything as value to them. Avoid both arrogance ("most aggressive X you'll find") and hedging. Conversational-professional beats formal-stiff for startups and smaller companies.

## Mission-driven shops
This is where cover letters matter most and pandering is most lethal. Mission-driven companies deliberately self-select for people who chose the mission, and they reward evidence of a prior choice over eloquent mission statements. Know the product line. Generic "I love tech and the mission" gets filtered. The tell they all reward is evidence of a prior choice, not a stated feeling: a specific contribution to a relevant program, a concrete resilience or failure story, genuine alignment shown through specifics rather than worship.

## Failure modes that get letters rejected
- Restating the resume in prose.
- Generic, one-size-fits-all.
- Vague competency claims instead of quantified outcomes.
- Making it about the candidate's wants, not what they will deliver.
- Pandering on mission with no evidence.
- AI-boilerplate tone. Most hiring managers notice; few like AI-written letters.
- Coordination described as impact; "I own the roadmap" with no decision shown.
- Fake metrics ("measurably improved" with no measure).

## Do cover letters even get read now?
Increasingly optional, not dead. They matter most for: mission-driven or smaller companies (they hire dozens, not hundreds, and decide on the letter); career or industry transitions (the "why" needs explaining); roles where written communication is the job (PM, leadership). They matter least at large-scale funnels where resume plus referral dominate.
